# Proposal Generator

Creates client-centric proposals that lead with the client's business problems, not the
firm's services. Each proposal frames the client's pain as the hero and the firm as the
guide, mapping pain to capability and packaging the work as a phased partnership journey
rather than a one-time vendor engagement. This skill is the **method**. The specifics it
operates on — the firm's own name, promise, service lines, and messaging — belong to one
organization, not to the skill. See [`ADAPT.md`](ADAPT.md) for what to decide before using
this for a given firm.

When those specifics are not yet decided, **do not invent them** — produce a clearly-labeled
**GENERIC DRAFT** with neutral `PLACEHOLDER` values (e.g. `[FIRM NAME]`, `[PROMISE]`,
`[SERVICE LINE]`) and list what's missing. **Never invent** the firm's name, promise, service
lines, prices, or outcome metrics. **All pricing and every outcome metric are HUMAN INPUT** —
never invent a number.

> **Core principle**: The client's pain is the hero. The firm is the guide.

## Firm & offering inputs

Before drafting or reviewing a proposal, you need a few firm-specific facts:

- **Who prepares the proposal** — the firm name, wordmark, and signature details.
- **Positioning** — the firm's promise, its key messages, and its differentiators.
- **Business model and service lines** — each service line's name, a plain-language summary,
  and the client pain it solves.

Resolve every firm-specific value (the firm name, exact wordmark, the promise, the key
messages, each service line) from the organization's own decisions — never hardcode them and
never invent them. If they have not been decided, see [`ADAPT.md`](ADAPT.md) and the Proposal
Intake in [`forms.md`](forms.md). **All pricing and every outcome metric are HUMAN INPUT.**

## Discovery inputs (per proposal)

A proposal also needs facts specific to **this client**, gathered in discovery. If they're
absent, ask the **Discovery Recap** questions in [`forms.md`](forms.md) before drafting:

- The client's pain, in their own words (quote them back).
- Current-state cost of that pain (time, errors, missed revenue) — client's numbers.
- Desired outcome and what "success" looks like to them.
- Constraints: timeline, budget signal, decision-makers, existing systems.

## When to Activate

**Triggers**:
- "Create a proposal for [client]" / "Draft a proposal for [project]"
- "Help me write a proposal"
- After a discovery call with a potential client
- When responding to an RFP
- When upselling or expanding an existing engagement

**Prerequisites**:
- Discovery information gathered (client pain points, current state, desired outcomes).
- The firm's identity, positioning, and service lines are decided (or the intake has been run).

## Client problem bundles (lead with pain)

Group the client's situation into one or more **problem bundles** — clusters of related
pain expressed in the client's own language. Lead with the bundle, then map it to the
firm's capability. Build the actual bundles from the firm's service lines (each service
line's client pain is the pain it addresses); the two below are illustrative shapes, not
a fixed catalog.

**Bundle shape — "information & decisions" pain**
> Client experience: "We're drowning in data, decisions take forever, no single source of truth."

| Pain point | What they say | Business impact |
|------------|---------------|-----------------|
| Scattered information | "Our data is everywhere" | Hours wasted, copy-paste errors |
| Manual reporting | "Someone spends days every week compiling reports" | Recurring labor cost, always outdated |
| Slow decisions | "By the time I get the numbers, the opportunity is gone" | Missed revenue, reactive posture |
| Low confidence | "I don't trust these numbers" | Decisions made on gut, not evidence |

**Bundle shape — "scale & repetition" pain**
> Client experience: "We can't scale, too many manual processes, the team is overwhelmed."

| Pain point | What they say | Business impact |
|------------|---------------|-----------------|
| Support overload | "Same questions asked a hundred ways" | Per-ticket cost, burnout |
| Knowledge silos | "Only one person knows how to do that" | Single points of failure |
| Manual processes | "Copying data between systems daily" | Errors, can't scale |
| Repetitive tasks | "Same thing over and over" | Low morale, turnover |

> **Outcome metrics are scenario-specific.** Express every benefit as a bracketed
> placeholder computed from the **client's own numbers** (e.g. `[X hours/week reclaimed]`,
> `[Y% error reduction]`, `[Z-month payback]`). Never carry a fixed figure between
> proposals.

## Phased engagement framework

Package the work as a phased journey that delivers quick wins early while building toward
lasting transformation. The four canonical phases are generic methodology:

```
ASSESS (1-2 wks) -> FOUNDATION (4-8 wks) -> VISIBILITY (3-6 wks) -> AUTOMATION (4-12 wks)
 (Discovery)        (Build the base)        (Make it visible)       (Add intelligence)
```

- **ASSESS** — deep dive, stakeholder interviews, current-state audit, prioritized roadmap.
- **FOUNDATION** — build the core platform/infrastructure, connect sources, baseline quality.
- **VISIBILITY** — define success metrics, build views/reports, enable self-service, train the team.
- **AUTOMATION** — layer intelligence and automation on the foundation; optimize continuously.

Adapt phase names and scope to the engagement, but **always start with ASSESS** — never
propose a big build before a discovery phase. Map each phase to the relevant service lines;
do not expose internal service codes.

## Pricing philosophy

- **Phase-based project pricing**, not hourly.
- **Retainer for the ongoing partnership** once the foundation is built.
- **Avoid hourly billing** unless the client explicitly requests it.
- **Value-based, not time-based** — price reflects business impact, never effort.
- **Every price is HUMAN INPUT** — the skill never fills a number.

## Anti-patterns (NEVER DO)

- Listing internal service codes in a client-facing proposal.
- Leading with "We offer…" instead of "You're facing…".
- Presenting a menu of services instead of a journey.
- Emphasizing features over outcomes.
- Using technical jargon the client doesn't use.
- Positioning the firm as a one-time project vendor.
- Sending a template without heavy per-client customization.
- Skipping the ASSESS phase.
- Inventing a price or an outcome metric.
