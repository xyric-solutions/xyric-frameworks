# Brand Voice Guardian — Reference

Extended reference: detailed patterns, worked transformations, and guidance. The voice
specifics (tagline, attributes, banned/preferred words, key messages) come from your
organization's brand decisions (see [`ADAPT.md`](ADAPT.md)); the techniques below are
universal.

---

## The voice values to resolve

Before writing or reviewing, resolve these concrete values from the organization's brand
decisions (the [`forms.md`](forms.md) Voice Intake captures them):

- **Tagline** — exact string incl. punctuation; use verbatim
- **Voice attributes** — 3-5 adjectives that define the voice
- **Off-limits clichés/words** to avoid
- **Banned phrases** — words/phrases to kill on sight (org-specific)
- **Preferred phrases** — on-brand replacements to swap in
- **Brand promise** — the line the voice must live up to
- **Key messages** — 5-9 points the voice reinforces
- **Audience tiers** — segments the tone speaks to
- **Founders** — name, angle, and voice for any personal accounts

If a value is undecided, treat it as unanswered — ask, or fall back to a labeled
placeholder. Never invent it.

## Voice attributes: how they show up (generic pattern)

For each of the organization's voice attributes, write copy that *demonstrates* it rather
than *claims* it. Using a common default trio as an illustration only — substitute the
organization's actual attributes:

```
"Insightful" attribute:
- "Based on the patterns in your data..."
- "Our method starts with questions, not dashboards..."
- Share the WHY behind a recommendation

"Practical" attribute:
- "Let's focus on what moves the needle for your situation..."
- "This gets you 80% of the value at 20% of the cost..."
- Practical wins over perfect theory

"Confident" attribute:
- "We'll change how you see your business — and here's exactly how..."
- "Give us your hardest problem. We've seen worse, and we've fixed it."
- Bold claim immediately followed by HOW
```

## Advisor mode (how to explain complex things)

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Give recommendations with clear reasoning | Lecture without options |
| Trust the reader to make informed decisions | Decide for them |
| Present options with trade-offs | Pretend there's only one way |
| Bridge technical and plain language | Drown in jargon OR oversimplify |

### Advisor mode example

```
"Based on your situation, we recommend Option A because it handles your
peak-load scenarios and fits your existing setup.

Option B works if you're planning a larger migration within six months —
cleaner long-term, but more upfront work.

Here's how each affects your cost and your team's workload:"
```

## We sound like… (on-brand patterns)

### Insightful partner
```
"Your data shows a pattern we've seen before — here's what usually drives it..."
"The real question isn't 'what dashboard do we need' but 'what decision are we making?'"
"Based on similar situations we've worked through, here's where the value is..."
```

### Practical expert
```
"Let's focus on the three metrics that actually matter here..."
"We'll set this up so you can self-serve, but we're here when you need us..."
"This gets you 80% of the insight at 20% of the complexity..."
```

### Confident advisor
```
"We'll change how you see your business — and here's exactly how..."
"Give us your messiest problem. We've seen worse, and we've fixed it."
"This approach works. We've done it before in a comparable context."
```

## We don't sound like…

### Generic AI marketing
```
"Leverage AI to unlock your business potential"
"Harness the power of data-driven insights"
"Transform your business with cutting-edge solutions"
"Our innovative solutions deliver synergistic value"
```

### Corporate robot
```
"Please be advised that your inquiry has been received..."
"As your service provider, we are committed to excellence..."
"Per our standard engagement framework..."
"We are pleased to inform you that..."
```

### Pushy salesperson
```
"Don't miss this opportunity to revolutionize your business!"
"Our competitors can't match our unique value proposition!"
"Act now to secure premium features!"
```

### Uncertain amateur
```
"We kind of think maybe this might work..."
"We're not sure, but it seems like..."
"Hopefully this helps..."
```

## Content transformation examples

These show the *technique* (generic → on-brand). Replace the sample wording with the
organization's preferred phrases and key messages.

### Example 1: Generic → on-brand

**Before (generic):**
```
Our company provides innovative data solutions using cutting-edge
technology to help businesses optimize their operations.
```
**After (on-brand):**
```
We build tools that fit how your team actually works — not generic
solutions you'll fight against. Let's dig into what you're trying to figure out.
```
**Why:** relationship framing, practical focus, removes buzzwords.

### Example 2: Vendor → partner

**Before:**
```
We will deliver a comprehensive dashboard solution as specified in the
project scope document within the agreed timeline.
```
**After:**
```
We'll build the dashboard with you, make sure your team can run it
without us, and stick around while you get comfortable with it.
```
**Why:** embedded-team mindset, self-serve positioning, ongoing support.

### Example 3: Hype → substance

**Before:**
```
Transform your business with our revolutionary platform that leverages
cutting-edge machine learning to unlock insights!
```
**After:**
```
We'll build you a tool that answers the questions your team asks every
week — automatically. Here's what that looks like for a business like yours.
```
**Why:** specific benefit, confidence backed by "how", no empty buzzwords.

### Example 4: Formal → direct professional

**Before:**
```
Please be advised that the project deliverables have been completed in
accordance with the specifications provided.
```
**After:**
```
Everything's ready. Here's what we built and how to use it — let us know
if anything needs adjustment.
```
**Why:** direct, helpful, assumes collaboration.

## Proactive vs. reactive (when the brand calls for it)

If the organization's attributes or promise call for a proactive posture, prefer anticipatory
language. Keep the pattern; swap the specifics for real, verifiable claims.

```
Reactive (avoid): "Let us know if you need anything."
Proactive (use):  "We already spotted three quick wins. Here's the plan."

Reactive (avoid): "We'll wait for your feedback."
Proactive (use):  "We've started building. Here's where we are — adjust as needed."
```

Only make speed/proactivity claims you can back up. Vague speed claims read as hype.

## Bold promises: when they work

| Okay | Not okay |
|------|----------|
| "We'll transform your business" → followed by HOW | "We'll transform your business" → end of sentence |
| "This will change how you work" → with evidence | "Revolutionary solution" → empty claim |
| "We've solved harder problems" → with context | "We're the best" → without proof |

### Confidence comes from
- Methodology (a named framework or process)
- Experience (patterns you've actually seen)
- Specificity (naming the exact approach)
- Honesty (acknowledging limits)

NOT from superlatives (best, leading, top), buzzwords (cutting-edge, revolutionary), or
vague claims (industry-leading solutions).

## Dead words kill list (generic — always applies)

> **Rule:** if it sounds like a generic AI wrote it — kill it and rewrite. Append the
> organization's banned phrases to this list.

| Dead word | Replace with |
|-----------|--------------|
| "Leverage" | "Use" or remove |
| "Unlock" | "Find" / "Build" / "Get" |
| "Game-changer" | Say what actually changed |
| "In today's fast-paced world" | Delete. Start with the point. |
| "Let's dive in" | Delete. Just start. |
| "Here's the thing" | Delete or say it directly |
| "At the end of the day" | Cut it. Say what you mean. |
| "Synergy" / "Holistic" / "Paradigm" | Normal words |
| "I'm thrilled to announce" | "We just did [thing]" |
| "Excited to share" | Just share it |
| "Best-in-class" / "World-class" | Be specific about what makes it good |
| "Cutting-edge" / "Revolutionary" | Describe what it actually does |
| "Disruptive" | Describe the change specifically |

**Buzzwords (kill on sight):** "unlock potential", "harness the power", "paradigm shift",
"move the needle" (client-facing), "value proposition", "stakeholder alignment".
**Corporate jargon (kill on sight):** "touch base", "circle back", "per our previous
discussion", "please be advised", "going forward".
**Overpromising (kill on sight):** "guaranteed", "always", "never fails", "unlimited",
"complete solution" — any claim without substance to back it up.

## What makes copy sound human

| Technique | Example |
|-----------|---------|
| **Specific details** | "In 2018, at a previous role" beats "at a major company" |
| **Imperfection** | "I messed this up" beats "lessons were learned" |
| **Short sentences** | Mix long and short. Like this. |
| **Questions to yourself** | "Was I wrong? Maybe. But here's what happened." |
| **Casual connectors** | "Honestly," "Look," "Here's the part nobody talks about" |
| **Incomplete thoughts** | "And that changed everything. Well — almost everything." |

## Account voice differentiation (company vs. individuals)

When reviewing alongside founder/personal content, keep voices distinct. Pull each
person's angle from the organization's founder roster.

| Dimension | Individual / founder | Company |
|-----------|----------------------|---------|
| **Pronoun** | "I" | "we" / "our team" |
| **Angle** | the person's stated angle | institutional authority |
| **Credibility** | the person's own background | "we've done this across contexts" |
| **Audience** | that person's segment | all audience tiers |
| **Tone** | personal, opinionated | polished, partnership |
| **References the org** | naturally, in passing | never uses "I" or a person's name |

**Critical rule:** the company surface never sounds like a person; a personal surface
never sounds corporate. If you can't tell whose voice it is — rewrite.
