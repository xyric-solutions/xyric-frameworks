# Brand Voice Guardian

Keep all external content sounding distinctly like one organization — consistent
personality, consistent words, consistent promise — and review existing work against
that voice. This skill is the **method** (how to enforce voice). The specifics it
operates on — the actual tagline, voice attributes, banned/preferred words, and key
messages — are an organization's, not part of the skill. See [`ADAPT.md`](ADAPT.md) for
what to decide before using this for a given organization.

When the voice specifics are not yet decided, **do not invent them** — produce a
clearly-labeled **GENERIC DRAFT** with neutral `PLACEHOLDER` voice attributes and a
generic kill-list, and list what's missing. Never invent the organization's tagline,
voice attributes, banned/preferred words, or key messages.

## What "on-brand voice" means

Voice = the consistent personality in writing. The organization's voice attributes
(typically 3-5 adjectives) define it. A common, strong default trio is **insightful,
practical, confident** — but use whatever the organization states, not these.

| Attribute (organization's) | What it asks of the copy |
|----------------------------|--------------------------|
| Attribute 1 | Is this claim backed by evidence / a pattern / a method? |
| Attribute 2 | Is this actionable and concrete for the reader? |
| Attribute 3 | Does this sound assured — bold but not arrogant? |

Match the copy to the organization's stated attributes; flag drift toward a generic,
interchangeable tone.

## Brand promise comes through in tone

The organization's tagline and brand promise are the proof the voice must live up to.
Use the tagline **exactly as written** (including punctuation); never modify,
abbreviate, or translate it. Every piece should sound like it came from a team that owns
its promise, not a generic agency.

## Partnership / relationship voice (not transactional voice)

For service- or relationship-led organizations, prefer collaborative framing over
transactional framing. Adapt the specific words to the organization, but keep the pattern:

| Prefer (collaborative) | Avoid (transactional) |
|------------------------|-----------------------|
| "Let's work through this together" | "We'll deliver this to you" |
| "Your team can run this" | "You'll need us for everything" |
| "Based on your context" | "Best practice says…" |
| "We're here as your team" | "As your service provider…" |
| "What does success look like for you?" | "Here's our standard package" |

If the organization is product-led rather than service-led, swap this for its own
relationship framing from its key messages.

## Both sides of the offer must show (if the org has more than one)

Many organizations do more than one thing (e.g. optimize the existing AND build the new;
advise AND execute). If the organization's key messages or business model describe
multiple sides, the voice must reflect each, not collapse to one. Don't let the copy
present the organization as only its most obvious line of work.

## Context-based tone

Tone shifts by surface while voice stays constant. Map the organization's attributes onto
each:

| Context | Tone lean |
|---------|-----------|
| Proposals / sales | Confident advisor |
| Product / technical docs | Clear, precise expert |
| Social / thought leadership | Insightful, point-first |
| Support / delivery | Practical partner |
| Website copy | Confident + practical |
| Internal comms | Direct + transparent |

## Language discipline (resolve specifics from the organization's word lists)

The categories below are universal; the **actual words** come from the organization's
banned phrases (kill on sight) and preferred phrases (swap in), plus its off-limits
clichés.

| Category | Use (preferred phrases) | Instead of (banned phrases) |
|----------|-------------------------|------------------------------|
| Relationship | the org's partner words | vendor / provider language |
| Expertise | method / pattern / approach words | "best practice", "industry standard" |
| Action | build / dig in / figure out | leverage, utilize, deploy |
| Results | concrete-impact words | synergy, optimization |
| Confidence | "works", "proven", "we've done this" | cutting-edge, revolutionary |

### Dead words (generic kill list — always applies)

Independent of any brand, kill AI-tell and empty-corporate phrasing and rewrite:

| Dead word / phrase | Replace with |
|--------------------|--------------|
| "Leverage" | "Use" or remove |
| "Unlock" | "Find" / "Build" / "Get" |
| "Game-changer" | Say what actually changed |
| "In today's fast-paced world" | Delete. Start with the point. |
| "Let's dive in" | Delete. Just start. |
| "Here's the thing" | Delete or say it directly |
| "At the end of the day" | Cut it. Say what you mean. |
| "Synergy" / "Holistic" / "Paradigm" | Normal words |
| "Best-in-class" / "World-class" | Be specific about what makes it good |
| "Cutting-edge" / "Revolutionary" / "Disruptive" | Describe what it actually does |
| "I'm thrilled to announce" | "We just did [thing]" |
| "Excited to share" | Just share it |

> If it sounds like a generic AI wrote it — kill it and rewrite. Append the
> organization's banned phrases to this list when reviewing.

## Bold promises: confidence with substance

Bold promises are fine **only** if followed by HOW:

- "We'll transform your business" + the method/evidence = OK
- "We'll transform your business" (end of sentence) = NOT OK

Confidence comes from **methodology, experience, specificity, honesty** — never from
superlatives, buzzwords, or vague claims.

## Distinct voices by account (company vs. individual)

When the organization has named founders/individuals who write under personal accounts,
keep company voice and individual voices distinct:

| Dimension | Company | Individual / founder |
|-----------|---------|----------------------|
| Pronoun | "we" / "our team" | "I" (personal) |
| Tone | institutional, polished, partnership | personal, opinionated, raw |
| Credibility | "we've done this across contexts" | the person's own background and angle |

Each person's angle and voice come from the organization's founder roster. The company
surface never uses "I" or a founder's name; a founder surface never sounds corporate. If
you can't tell whose voice it is — rewrite.

## Anti-patterns

| Don't | Do instead |
|-------|------------|
| Sound like generic AI output | Sound distinctly like this org — per its attributes |
| Use buzzwords without substance | Back claims with specifics |
| Position as a transactional vendor | Position per the org's relationship framing |
| Make empty promises | Make bold claims + show how |
| Over-explain or over-hedge | Be direct and confident |
| Use dead words / the org's banned phrases | Use the human / preferred-phrase alternatives |
| Present a multi-sided offer as one thing | Show every side the org describes |
| Sound reactive or passive | Sound proactive where the brand calls for it |
| Modify or abbreviate the tagline | Use the tagline verbatim |
