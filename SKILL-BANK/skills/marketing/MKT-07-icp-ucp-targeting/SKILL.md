# ICP & UCP Targeting

The **targeting method** for all marketing work: pick the right audience tier, lead with
their pain, and reinforce a competitive-positioning pillar in every artifact. This skill is
the **method** — segmentation, qualification, messaging-by-tier, and campaign coverage. The
specifics it operates on — your actual ICP tiers, UCP pillars, key messages, and
differentiators — are decided per organization. See [`ADAPT.md`](ADAPT.md) for what to
decide before using this for a given organization.

When those specifics are not yet decided, **do not invent them** — produce a clearly-labeled
**GENERIC DRAFT** with neutral `PLACEHOLDER` tier names (e.g. `Tier A / Tier B`) and pillar
placeholders, and list what's missing. **Never invent** ICP tiers, UCP pillars, key messages,
the brand promise, or pricing. Tier definitions describe **who and what pain**, never a dollar
figure.

## The two axes

Every targeting decision answers two questions:

1. **WHO** — which of your ICP tiers is this for? (the audience axis)
2. **WHY US** — which of your UCP pillars does it prove? (the differentiation axis)

A piece of work that nails one axis but ignores the other is half-targeted. Aim for both.

## ICP: the audience axis

- The **tiers** are your organization's named audience segments (each has a name + description).
  Reference them by their real names, not invented labels.
- **Shared psychographics** — traits every tier holds in common — describe mindset, not
  budget. Capture them once and reuse across all tiers. See [`reference.md`](reference.md) for
  a generic psychographic frame to adapt.
- **Anti-patterns** — the audiences the org deliberately does NOT serve. Qualify these out
  early; a culture mismatch kills engagements. Keep them with your positioning notes.

> Tier definitions are about *who they are* and *what they're trying to do* — firmographics
> (size, role, industry) and psychographics (mindset, triggers). Never bake pricing into a
> tier definition in a shareable artifact.

## UCP: the differentiation axis

- The **pillars** are your competitive-positioning claims — each is a name, a one-line
  meaning, and a concrete **proof point**. If you have no formal pillars yet, use your
  differentiators as the pillar set.
- Every published artifact should reinforce **at least one** pillar with its proof — not a
  vague claim. "We're fast" is a claim; the matching proof point makes it credible.
- Across a campaign or quarter, **all** pillars should get airtime — track this in the
  coverage audit (see [`forms.md`](forms.md)).

## Key messages

- Your **key messages** are the canonical, pre-approved messages. **Use them verbatim**
  (including punctuation) in the contexts noted for each.
- Lead with the prospect's **pain**, then bridge to the message — never open with a list of
  capabilities.

## Messaging by tier

Same topic, different angle per tier. For each ICP tier, define:
- the **message that resonates** (uses the tier's own vocabulary and pain), and
- the **message that doesn't** (the off-key register to avoid — e.g. enterprise language to a
  solo founder, or speed-only pitch to a depth-seeking buyer).

The tier-by-tier messaging table and a worked "one topic, four angles" example are in
[`reference.md`](reference.md). Fill it from your tiers.

## How to Use This Skill

### For content generation
1. Identify the target ICP tier before writing anything.
2. Select messaging that resonates with **that** tier — not generic.
3. Reinforce at least one UCP pillar, with its proof point.
4. Emit the targeting metadata (tier + pillar + key message #) in the content output format.

### For lead qualification
1. Check the prospect against the ICP tier definitions (firmographic + psychographic match).
2. Verify they don't match an anti-pattern.
3. Identify which UCP pillar is their biggest **decision trigger**.
4. Route to the appropriate sales approach. Use the checklist in [`forms.md`](forms.md).

### For campaign planning
1. Map each campaign to a primary ICP tier (and any secondary tiers).
2. Ensure campaign messaging uses tier-appropriate language.
3. Verify the UCP pillars represented across the campaign.
4. Check that every tier gets attention across the quarter (coverage audit in `forms.md`).

## Anti-Patterns

| Don't | Instead |
|-------|---------|
| Write "one-size-fits-all" messaging | Tailor to a specific ICP tier |
| Lead with your own capabilities | Lead with the prospect's pain point, then bridge |
| Use one tier's vocabulary for another tier | Match each tier's register and language |
| Ignore UCP in content | Every post reinforces at least one pillar with its proof |
| Forget the smallest/individual tier | Give every defined tier regular coverage |
| Pursue anti-pattern prospects | Politely pass — culture mismatch kills projects |
| Bake a price into the targeting artifact | Keep tiers about who/pain; pricing lives elsewhere |
