# Competitive Analysis — Reference

Extended methodology for competitive analysis. The "our position" framing is decided per
organization (see [`ADAPT.md`](ADAPT.md)); the methods and depth tiers below are universal.

---

## The "our position" framing (decided per organization)

The `US` / "Our Opportunity" content draws on a small set of our-side values:

- **Promise** — our core value statement
- **Key messages** — the promises we lead with
- **Differentiators** — what makes us genuinely different
- **Target segments** — who we serve
- **Business model** — how we make money (and service lines or product taxonomy, as relevant)

These are decided per organization — gather them via the Our-Position Intake in
[`forms.md`](forms.md) and use them at analysis time. If a value isn't settled, treat it as
unanswered (use a labeled `PLACEHOLDER`). The competitor-side research never needs them.

---

## Research Methodology

1. **Multi-source approach** — always cross-reference findings across several sources:
   the competitor's website, social media, customer reviews, industry/analyst reports,
   pricing pages, changelogs, and job postings.
2. **Regular cadence** — quarterly for fast-moving markets, semi-annually for stable ones.
3. **Living document** — competitive analysis is updated, not recreated each time; keep a
   change log and date every refresh.

---

## Identifying Hidden Competitors

- Check job postings for companies hiring in your space.
- Monitor industry conference speaker and sponsor lists.
- Track venture capital investment in adjacent spaces.
- Follow industry analysts and their coverage.
- Review patent filings in relevant technology areas.
- Watch review-site "compared to" and "alternatives to" pages.

## Analysis Depth by Competitor Type

| Type | Recommended Depth |
|------|-------------------|
| Direct (top 3) | Full 5P analysis, quarterly updates |
| Direct (others) | Feature comparison, semi-annual review |
| Indirect | High-level positioning, annual review |
| Potential / substitutes | Watch list, trigger-based monitoring |

## Strategic Positioning Insights

When analyzing a competitor's positioning, look for:

- **Messaging patterns** — what words do they emphasize repeatedly?
- **Audience targeting** — who, specifically, are they addressing?
- **Value proposition** — what specific outcome do they promise?
- **Differentiation claims** — what do they say makes them unique?
- **Pricing psychology** — how do they frame their pricing (anchor, tiers, "from $X")?
- **Proof signals** — case studies, logos, certifications, integrations they lead with.

---

## Feature Benchmarking

Build a feature matrix where rows are capabilities that matter to the buyer and columns
are competitors plus `US` (our decided position). Rules:

- Mark Y / N / Partial — never leave a cell blank; "unknown" is itself a finding.
- Weight features by buyer importance, not by how many competitors have them.
- A feature everyone has is table stakes, not a differentiator.
- A feature nobody has, that buyers want, is the opportunity — flag it.

---

## Positioning Maps

A 2x2 with axes that genuinely discriminate in this market (price vs. depth, breadth vs.
specialization, self-serve vs. high-touch). Plot every competitor and `US`. The
uncontested quadrant is usually where the opportunity lives — but verify demand exists
there before recommending it.

---

## SWOT Discipline

- **Strengths / Weaknesses** are internal (about us) — pull strengths from our
  differentiators; be honest about weaknesses.
- **Opportunities / Threats** are external (about the market) — derived from the
  competitor findings, not from us.
- Each SWOT entry should map to a recommendation or it doesn't belong.

---

## From Analysis to Action

Every competitive analysis ends in prioritized, rationalized recommendations — not a
data dump. For each recommended action state: the action, the gap it exploits, the
evidence behind it, and the expected effect. Tie the action back to our differentiators so
the move reinforces what already makes us different.
