# Cold Outreach

Write cold outreach emails that get opened, get read, and get a reply. This skill is
the **method** — hook-first structure, the human check, the dead-words kill list, and a
follow-up cadence. The specifics it operates on — the organization's name, positioning,
differentiators, voice, and service lines — are decided per organization, not part of the
skill. See [`ADAPT.md`](ADAPT.md) for what to decide before using this for a given
organization.

Every email must have a hook that catches the eye, be scannable in 30 seconds, carry
results computed from the prospect's own situation, and sound like a real person wrote it
on their laptop — never AI-polished.

When the organization specifics are not yet decided, **do not invent them** — produce a
clearly-labeled **GENERIC DRAFT** with neutral, obviously-non-real placeholders
(`[ORG NAME]`, `[ONE-LINER]`, `[DIFFERENTIATOR]`) and list what's missing. **Never invent**
the org's name, tagline, differentiators, founder names, results metrics, pricing, or
service codes.

## Core Principles

### The 5 Rules of Cold Outreach

| Rule | Why |
|------|-----|
| **Hook first** | The subject line and first sentence must make them stop. No "I hope this finds you well." Ever. |
| **Scannable** | Short paragraphs, bullet points, bold headers. If they can't get the gist in 30 seconds — too long. |
| **Results-backed** | Concrete numbers in every email — but computed from the *prospect's* situation, never fixed boilerplate. Percentages, hours saved, timelines. No vague "we deliver great results." |
| **Human voice** | Must sound like a real person wrote it — conversational, direct, slightly informal. Never AI-polished. Match the org's voice attributes. |
| **The org block is premium** | The "who we are in N seconds" block must be outstanding and specific. Assemble it from the org's one-liner + differentiators — never generic. |

### The Org Identity in Cold Emails

Assemble the "who we are in N seconds" block at draft time — never ship a hardcoded one:

> **Tagline**: the org's tagline (use verbatim, including punctuation; never modify or translate).
> **Descriptor**: the org's one-line "what we are."
> **One-liner**: the org's elevator description.

Build the bullet block from the org's differentiators and service lines, mapped to the
service the email is offering. If the org has more than one side to its model (e.g.
optimize-existing vs. build-from-zero), pick the side that matches the prospect.

## Email Structure (Mandatory)

Every cold outreach email follows this structure:

```
1. SUBJECT LINE
   → Value-first. Question or specific benefit. Never corporate.
   → Examples: "Can I save your team [X] hours a week?"
               "Your data pipeline — [X] days, not [X] months"

2. PERSONALIZED OPENER (1-2 sentences)
   → Show you've done research on their company
   → Reference something specific about them
   → Ask a question that makes them think about their own problem

3. PAIN VALIDATION (1-2 sentences)
   → State the pain point with a specific number/stat for companies their size
   → Connect it to companies like theirs

4. WHO WE ARE IN N SECONDS (bullet block)
   → 3-4 bullet points — what makes the org different
   → Pull from the org's differentiators + the matched service line
   → Adapt per scenario (which service line is being offered)

5. PERSONALIZED VALUE (bullet block)
   → "What we'd look at for [Company]:"
   → 2-3 specific things relevant to their business
   → Shows research and intent, not a generic pitch

6. RESULTS (bullet block)
   → Numbers computed from the prospect's own inputs — NEVER fixed boilerplate
   → Bracketed placeholders [X]% / [X] hours / [timeframe] until you have real figures
   → Always at the end — this is the punchline

7. CTA (1-2 sentences)
   → Reframe the call as value, not an ask
   → "10 minutes of your time — and I'll show you exactly where those [X] hours are going."
   → Never: "Would you be available for a quick call?"

8. SIGN-OFF
   → First name only (the person signing the email)
   → Role + org name
   → No "Best regards" or "Kind regards" — too formal for cold email
```

## Results discipline (read this twice)

Results are the punchline — and the single most dangerous place to leak a fake number.

- **Compute every metric from the prospect's own situation.** Ask for or estimate their
  current hours/cost/volume, then derive the saving. A percentage with no basis is noise.
- **Never ship a fixed percentage or timeframe as if it's a guarantee.** Use bracketed
  placeholders (`[X]%`, `[X] hours/week`, `[timeframe]`) until a real, defensible figure
  exists for *this* prospect or a genuinely comparable engagement.
- **Only use results you can back up.** If a specific number doesn't exist yet, use
  directional language: "companies like yours typically see [range]."
- **No invented case studies, client names, or product names.** Reference comparable work
  only in generic, non-identifying terms unless the org has authorized a named reference.

## The Human Check (Mandatory Before Sending)

| # | Check | If Fail → |
|---|-------|-----------|
| 1 | Does the subject line make you want to open? | Rewrite — needs a hook |
| 2 | Does the opener mention THEM, not us? | Flip — lead with their company/problem |
| 3 | Can you scan it in 30 seconds? | Cut — too many paragraphs, too wordy |
| 4 | Are there concrete numbers, computed from their situation? | Add — but never fixed boilerplate metrics |
| 5 | Does it sound like a human wrote it on their laptop? | Kill AI phrasing — no "I'm reaching out," no "I hope this finds you well" |
| 6 | Is the org block premium and specific? | Rewrite — should feel like no other company could say this |
| 7 | Is there a Dead Word? | Kill it (see Dead Words list) |
| 8 | Would a competitor be able to send this exact email? | Rewrite — add the org's specific differentiators |

## Dead Words in Cold Emails (Kill on Sight)

| Dead Word | Why It's Dead | Use Instead |
|-----------|--------------|-------------|
| "I hope this finds you well" | Every cold email starts with this | Skip it. Start with the hook. |
| "I'm reaching out because" | Announces it's a cold email | Just say the thing |
| "Partnership opportunity" | Generic subject line, screams spam | Value-based subject |
| "Comprehensive solutions" | Means nothing | Be specific about what you build |
| "End-to-end" | Overused buzzword | Name the actual services |
| "Data-driven insights" | Vague and AI-sounding | Name the concrete outcome |
| "Leverage" / "Unlock" | Tired buzzwords | "Use" — or just be specific |
| "Best regards" / "Kind regards" | Too formal | Just your name |
| "Would you be available" | Passive, weak | "When works?" |
| "I'd love to connect" | LinkedIn noise | State the value, then ask |

Also kill anything on the org's "words to avoid" list.

## "Who we are in N seconds" — assemble per service line

Build this block at draft time, one variant per offered service. For each service-line
entry, write 3-4 bullets that combine:

- **What it is** — from the service line's summary.
- **Speed proof** — the org's speed differentiator, stated as a concrete proof point, not
  a vague claim.
- **Ownership / fit** — what the prospect keeps or how it's tailored to them.
- **Who they're talking to** — the human angle (founder-led, embedded, etc.) from the org's
  differentiators or business model.

Generic shape (fill from the org's decided values — do NOT hardcode):

```
- [Service-line summary — what we build/optimize, stated as our specialization]
- [Speed proof — e.g. "First quote in [X] hours. [Deliverable] in [timeframe]"]
- [Ownership/fit point — e.g. "You own everything" or "custom-built for your workflows"]
- [Human angle — e.g. "Founder-led: you're talking to me, not a sales team"]
```

See [`reference.md`](reference.md) for full per-scenario template skeletons.

## Anti-Patterns

| Don't | Do Instead |
|-------|-----------|
| Start with "I'm reaching out from [org]" | Start with THEIR problem or a question |
| Write more than 200 words | Keep it under 150 if possible |
| Use two back-to-back bullet lists without a break | Separate with a bold header for each block |
| Put the best number in the middle | Results go at the END — it's the punchline |
| Ship a fixed result metric (e.g. a fixed %) | Compute it from the prospect's numbers; use brackets until then |
| Use a generic category word the org rejects | Describe what the org actually builds/does |
| Write a CTA that sounds like an ask | Write a CTA that sounds like value |
| Send without personalizing | Every email must mention the company name and something specific |
| Invent a client name, case study, or product | Reference comparable work generically; name only what the org authorizes |
