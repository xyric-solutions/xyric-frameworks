# Social Content (Twitter/X) — Reference

Loaded on demand for detailed patterns, examples, and guidance. The voice, pillars,
audience, and key messages are decided per organization (see [`ADAPT.md`](ADAPT.md)). The
frameworks and formats below are universal; the example **topics** are neutral illustrations
— replace them with your own pillars.

> North star: the organization's tagline and one-line descriptor. Mirror their energy;
> never paraphrase or substitute them.

## Content Strategy Quick Reference (X/Twitter)

### 60/30/10 Content Mix on X

| Bucket | % | X Examples |
|--------|---|-----------|
| **Core** | 60% | Pillar-aligned hot takes, contrarian insights, methodology threads, pattern stories |
| **Personality** | 30% | Quick reactions, founder/team banter, real talk, reflective weekend takes |
| **Wildcard** | 10% | Off-topic takes, trending reactions, random polls, analogies from outside your field |

> If your org uses a different ratio, record it in your content brief and use that instead.

### Format Diversity Rule

Don't tweet the same format back-to-back. Formats for X:

| Format | Energy | Mix Bucket |
|--------|--------|------------|
| Hot Take | Bold, direct | Core |
| Insight Drop / One-liner | Sharp, concise | Core |
| Thread (Hot Take Deep-Dive) | Detailed contrarian | Core |
| Thread (Pattern Story) | Credibility, case study | Core |
| Thread (Framework Share) | Educational | Core |
| Industry React / QRT | Timely, opinionated | Core |
| Debate Starter Thread | Provocative, engaging | Core / Personality |
| Poll | Quick engagement | Personality / Wildcard |
| Quick Take | Punchy observation | Core |
| Weekend Reflective | Thoughtful, chill | Personality |
| Week Wrap | Summary, consistency | Personality |
| Off-Topic / Trending | Surprise, relatability | Wildcard |

### Audience Targeting for X Posts

Every tweet should naturally speak to at least one tier. Take your audience tiers and
write a short, X-style message for each:

| Per audience tier | What to write |
|-------------------|---------------|
| Tier name | The segment you're speaking to |
| Tier description | The pain or context that segment has |
| → X-style line | One sharp sentence that lands for that segment |

### Key-Message Pillars for X

Weave at least one of your differentiators (or key messages) into every post. For each
differentiator, write a single confident X-style one-liner that compresses it. Keep the
claim defensible — back it with experience, not hype.

### Human Check (mandatory before every tweet)

| # | Check | If Fail |
|---|-------|---------|
| 1 | Sounds like a real person — not generic AI copy? | Rewrite sharper, more casual |
| 2 | At least ONE specific detail? | Add a number, name, or place |
| 3 | Would you actually tweet this from a personal account? | Cut the corporate feel |
| 4 | First line punchy enough to stop the scroll? | Rewrite hook — bolder |
| 5 | FREE of Dead Words and `voice.avoid` terms? | Kill them all |

### Dead Words (never use)

| Dead Word | Replace With |
|-----------|-------------|
| "Leverage" | "Use" or remove |
| "Unlock" | "Find" / "Build" / "Get" |
| "Game-changer" | Say what actually changed |
| "In today's fast-paced world" | Delete |
| "Let's dive in" | Delete. Just start. |
| "Synergy" / "Holistic" / "Paradigm" | Normal words |
| "Excited to share" | Just share it |

Also kill anything on the organization's own avoid list.

### Founder / Team Content on X

If the organization has named founder/team voices, draft each person in their stated angle
and voice:

| Dimension | Source |
|-----------|--------|
| Style | The lens this person posts from (their angle) |
| Voice | Their tone |
| Best formats | Match formats to the angle (contrarian threads, one-liners, reflectives) |
| Collab | Tag-team threads — one founder opens an angle, another adds a complementary one |

If there are no named founder voices, post only in the organization voice (we, not I).

## Content Pillars (Twitter-Adapted Method)

Run the same pillars you use on long-form social — your own content pillars — but execute
sharper for X. For each pillar:

| Step | What to produce |
|------|-----------------|
| **Long-form style** | The measured, structured version of the thesis |
| **Twitter style** | The same thesis compressed to one contrarian, scroll-stopping line |
| **Thread angle** | The deeper argument a thread would make for that pillar |

> The example below is a **neutral illustration of the compression move** — not your content.
> Swap in your own content pillars.

**Illustration (generic topic):**
- *Long-form style*: "Many teams adopt a tool before defining the problem it should solve."
- *Twitter style*: "Most teams buy the tool first and define the problem never. Backwards."
- *Thread angle*: Why tool adoption rarely equals outcomes.

Apply this same compression to each item in your content pillars.

## Hot Takes Framework (Detailed)

### What Makes a Good Hot Take

| Good | Bad |
|------|-----|
| Contrarian but defensible | Controversial for attention |
| Based on real experience | Pure opinion without basis |
| Makes you think differently | Obvious or universally agreed |
| Invites discussion | Shuts down conversation |
| Confident, not arrogant | Attacking people or companies |

### Hot Take Formula

```
[Contrarian observation]

[Why most people think the opposite]

[What we've seen/learned]

[The implication]
```

### Strong Hot Take Example (structure illustration — neutral topic)

```
Hot take: "best practices" are usually just
the average of what everyone else already gave up trying to improve.

The teams that win audit the practice, not adopt it.
```

### Weak Hot Take (don't do)

```
Unpopular opinion: this thing is overhyped

Most people don't need it.

Agree?
```

Why it's weak: the "unpopular opinion" is actually the popular opinion (dishonest framing),
it has no specific basis, and it begs for engagement.

## Tweet Structure Formulas (Detailed)

### Single Tweet Formulas

**Pattern 1: Observation + Implication**
```
[What we see happening]

[What this actually means]
```

**Pattern 2: Wrong vs. Right**
```
Wrong: [Common approach]
Right: [Better approach]

[One sentence on why]
```

**Pattern 3: Counter-intuitive Truth**
```
[Bold statement that sounds wrong]

[Brief explanation of why it's true]
```

**Pattern 4: The Reframe**
```
Everyone talks about [common framing].

The real issue is [actual problem].
```

### Thread Opener Formulas

**Pattern 1: Hot Take Setup**
```
[Controversial statement]

Here's why — and what to do about it:

[thread continues]
```

**Pattern 2: Pattern Reveal**
```
A pattern we've seen [X] times:

[Brief setup]

Thread:
```

**Pattern 3: Framework Intro**
```
[Problem statement]

There's a framework that fixes this:

[thread continues]
```

## Full Tweet Examples

> These examples illustrate **form and rhythm only** — the topics are deliberately neutral.
> Replace every topic with one from your content pillars and ground each claim in your
> differentiators.

### Example 1: Single Tweet — Hot Take

```
Hot take: most "transformation" projects fail
because nobody could name the decision they'd make differently after.

You can't transform an outcome you never defined.

Define the decision first. The rest is decoration.
```

### Example 2: Single Tweet — Observation

```
Everyone says they want to "be more [aspiration]."

Most can't tell you what that changes on Monday morning.

The gap isn't ambition. It's translation.
```

### Example 3: Single Tweet — Reframe

```
Wrong: "We need [the deliverable]."
Right: "What decision will we make differently because of it?"

Start there. Everything else is decoration.
```

### Example 4: Single Tweet — Industry React

```
The tools have been around for years.

Most teams still decide by gut feeling
and the loudest voice in the room.

The tools were never the problem.
```

### Example 5: Thread — Pattern Story (structure illustration)

```
1/ A pattern we've seen multiple times:

Team has a "[X] problem."
Leadership wants the shiny solution.
Everyone is buried in manual work.

But the real problem is almost never what they think.

[thread continues]

2/ What they say:
"We need a better [deliverable]"
"We want [trendy capability]"
"Can you build us [the platform]?"

What we hear:
"Something is broken and we're not sure what."

3/ Step 1: We don't build anything.

We ask: "What can't you do today that you need to?"

Usually silence. Then the real conversation starts.

4/ The pattern:
- The work lives in too many places
- Nobody owns the source of truth
- Everything is stitched together by hand
- People wait days for stale information

This isn't a [deliverable] problem. It's a flow problem.

5/ The fix isn't flashy:
- Consolidate the sources
- Automate the manual stitching
- Build for the decision, not the display

No magic. Just fixing the plumbing.

6/ The result:
- Days of waiting → minutes
- Leadership sees on Monday what used to take until Friday
- The team freed up for real work, not wrangling

7/ The lesson:

Before adding intelligence, add infrastructure.

Most "[X] problems" are actually flow problems.

Fix those first. Everything else follows.
```

### Example 6: Thread — Framework (structure illustration)

```
1/ "We need [the deliverable]."

This is the wrong first sentence.

Here's the right one — and the framework that follows:

[thread continues]

2/ The right first sentence:

"What decision will we make differently because of this?"

If the answer is unclear, you haven't defined the problem yet.

3/ The Question-First Framework:

Before building anything, answer these:

4/ Question 1: What decision?

Not "what do you want to see?"
But "what will you DO with this?"

Specific. Actionable. Tied to a real moment.

5/ Question 2: What changes the decision?

What would make you choose A vs B?
That defines what you actually need.

Everything else is noise.

6/ Question 3: Who and when?

Who needs this? In what context?
Planning vs. real-time = different designs.

7/ The result:

[Deliverables] that get used.
Because they were built for decisions, not display.

Start with the question.
The rest builds itself.
```

## Anonymized Case Study Tweets

When sharing client/customer stories, anonymize fully (no names, no identifying detail) and
focus on the pattern and outcome.

### Short Format (Single Tweet)

```
We helped a team cut a multi-day process down to minutes.

The fix wasn't a fancy tool. It was fixing how the work flowed.

Most "[X] problems" are plumbing problems.
```

### Thread Format

```
Tweet 1:
A growing team asked us to "fix [the deliverable]."
But that wasn't the problem.
Here's what actually was — and the fix:
[thread continues]

Tweet 2:
What we saw:
- Days to compile routine work
- Decisions made on stale information
- A team stuck doing things by hand

Tweet 3:
What they asked for:
"Better [deliverable]" / "More [feature]" / "[Trendy capability]"

What they actually needed:
Clean flow.

Tweet 4:
The real problem:
The work lived in too many places. Nothing talked to each other.
Every output was manual stitching.
No amount of [deliverables] fixes broken plumbing.

Tweet 5:
The fix:
- One source of truth
- Automated flows
- Decision-focused outputs
Not flashy. But it works.

Tweet 6:
The result:
- Same work, automated
- Leadership sees on Monday what they used to wait until Friday
- The team freed up hours every week

Tweet 7:
The lesson:
Before asking "what tool do we need?"
ask "where does the work actually live?"
Most "[X] problems" are infrastructure problems in disguise.
```

## Engagement Guidelines

### Responding to Replies
- Reply quickly (Twitter rewards fast engagement)
- Add value, don't just thank
- Engage in genuine discussion
- Don't get defensive on pushback
- Quote tweet good replies with additional insight

### Proactive Engagement
- Quote tweet interesting takes with perspective
- Reply thoughtfully to industry threads
- Engage with potential customers' content before they're customers
- Build relationships through value, not pitches

### What Not to Do
- Don't argue with trolls
- Don't pitch in replies
- Don't self-promote in others' threads
- Don't engage with drama
